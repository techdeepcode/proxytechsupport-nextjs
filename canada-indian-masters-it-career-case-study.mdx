## Why This Keeps Happening to Indian Graduates in Canada

Before the cases — the context that nobody tells you before you land.

Canadian employers do not care about your GPA. They do not care about your 
university brand. What they screen for in the first 30 seconds of a technical 
interview is something that Indian university curricula almost never teach:

**How you communicate a technical decision under pressure, in Canadian workplace style.**

Not just the correct answer. The structured answer. The "here is my reasoning, 
here is the trade-off I considered, here is what I would do in production" answer.

Indian engineers often know the solution. They lose the interview because the 
*delivery* does not match what a Toronto or Vancouver hiring manager expects.

That gap — not your skills, not your degree — is what kept these four people 
in survival jobs for months.

## Case 1 — Arjun, 27. AWS Cloud. 14 Months at $17/hr in a Warehouse.

**Background:** Arjun finished his Master's in Computer Science at a Toronto 
university in late 2024. Back in India he had 2 years of AWS and Python 
experience at a mid-size IT company. His PGWP was valid for 3 years.

**What happened:** He applied to 200+ roles on LinkedIn and Indeed over 
8 months. Got 11 interview calls. Failed every technical round.

The feedback was always vague — "not the right fit," "looking for more 
Canadian experience." He started taking warehouse shifts to cover rent 
while continuing to apply.

**The actual problem we found:** When we ran a mock AWS Solutions Architect 
interview with him, his technical knowledge was solid — 85th percentile 
for the role. But when asked "walk me through a multi-region failover 
architecture," he dumped every AWS service name he knew in one paragraph, 
with no structure.

Canadian interviewers use the STAR format and expect it back. They want: 
situation → constraint → decision → outcome. Arjun was giving: 
solution → solution → solution.

**What we did:** Three sessions on structured technical communication. 
One full mock AWS interview with live feedback. Rebuilt his LinkedIn to 
target mid-market Canadian SaaS companies, not the FAANG roles he had 
been chasing.

**Result:** Offer in 6 weeks. AWS Cloud Engineer at a Mississauga fintech. 
$94,000 base. Full benefits. LMIA-exempt role.

> *"I had the skills for 14 months. I just did not know how to show them 
> in a way Canadian interviewers could evaluate."*

## The Pattern Across All Four Cases

These are not outliers. Every week we work with Indian graduates in Canada 
who have the same profile: strong technical fundamentals, real experience 
or strong academic projects, and a job search that is going nowhere.

The common thread is never a skills gap. It is always one of these three things:

**1. Resume positioning.** Indian IT resumes are written for Indian hiring 
managers. Canadian hiring managers read differently. The same experience, 
repositioned, produces completely different results.

**2. Interview communication style.** Technical correctness is necessary 
but not sufficient in Canada. Structure, narration, and stakeholder-awareness 
in your answers are what separate offers from rejections.

**3. Profile invisibility.** LinkedIn keyword optimization for the Canadian 
market is a different exercise than what works in India or globally. 
Recruiters at RBC, Shopify, Telus, and Canadian SaaS companies search 
with very specific filters.

## The PGWP Clock Is Real. The Window Is Shorter Than You Think.

If you finished your Master's in 2024 or early 2025, you are now 
12–18 months into your PGWP.

A 3-year PGWP sounds like a long time until you realize:
- 6 months of job searching is normal before a first real offer
- 6 months at a survival job to pay rent while searching is common
- Canadian Express Entry CRS points require a full-time NOC-0/A job offer
- Time spent in non-relevant work can affect your PR pathway

Every month in a survival job is not neutral. It is a month that is not 
counting toward your Canadian experience hours for immigration purposes.

This is not written to cause panic. It is written because most people 
realize this 18 months in — not 3 months in — and the earlier you 
address the interview problem, the more runway you have.
